# The Weight of Metal

A small collection of poems about forged things, struck things, and the
stubborn persistence of metal in human life.

---

## I. Sonnet for a Wedding Ring

A ring of gold, a circle without end,
Pressed warm against the knuckle's quiet bone,
Holds vows that years and seasons cannot bend —
A whispered promise wrought in solid tone.
The metal does not flinch when fingers age,
Nor flee when grief comes knocking at the door;
It bears its silent witness on the page
Of every quarrel, every kitchen floor.
What goldsmith, hammering at break of day,
Could know whose hand would wear his patient art?
Whose tears would slick the band? Whose wrinkled clay
Would carry it to ground, still near the heart?
  Outliving us, it slips into a drawer —
  A small bright zero, asking nothing more.

---

## II. Three Haiku for a Ring of Keys

Brass teeth in my palm —
each one opens a small life,
each one shuts it too.

The key I have lost
still hangs somewhere in the world.
A door waits, patient.

Pocket weight at dusk:
metal remembers the rooms
my hands have forgotten.

---

## III. The Anchor

Down it goes,
through the green and then the black,
spitting bubbles like a prayer
losing its words.

It does not ask why we stop
here, or here.
It simply takes the floor of the world
in its iron mouth
and bites down.

Above, the boat thinks it is still moving —
the wind insists.
Below, the anchor knows better.
It has met the bottom.
It has chosen its silt.

A hundred years from now,
when the boat is splinters,
when the captain is salt,
the anchor will still be there:
hosting barnacles,
holding nothing,
holding fast.

## IV. Coins

A coin is just a small round lie
We've all agreed to call the truth —
A flake of metal, milled and dry,
That stands in for our age and youth.

It buys the bread, it buys the wine,
It buys the hour the lover stays;
It will not buy the slope of pine
That marks the end of all our days.

But still we love its little weight,
Its profile of some long-dead king,
Who, careless of our every fate,
Is stamped upon this trivial thing.

So drop it, spin it, let it fall —
Heads or tails, the verdict's clear:
The coin outlives us, after all.
The coin was never really here.

## V. The Sword Speaks

I was a plow before I was a problem.
A man with a hammer and a furnace
beat the field out of me
and beat a question in.

Now I hang on a wall
above a mantelpiece
in a country that has forgotten its wars,
polished by a grandson
who has never bled.

I am not nostalgic.
I am not sorry.
I am a long thin argument
about who gets to keep the harvest —
and the argument has not ended.
Only the language has changed.

---

## VI. Five Haiku for Bells

The bronze bell is struck.
Somewhere a village remembers
it has a center.

Cracked bell in the field —
the cow walks past it, indifferent.
The cow is correct.

Ship's bell in the fog:
a small bright fact suspended
in the soup of dusk.

Temple bell at dawn —
the monk strikes it once. The world
reluctantly wakes.

Sleigh bells, then silence,
then the sound of the runner
hissing on snow again.

## VII. My Grandfather's Toolbox

*(blank verse)*

My grandfather's tools sit heavy in the box.
The hammer's grip is darkened where his palm
spent forty winters teaching it the shape
of work. The wrench is older than my name.
The level holds its small green eye of truth —
a bubble that has outlived the man who bought it,
the house he built with it, the wife he loved
beneath the roof he raised. I lift the saw
and feel his weight come into me through steel,
through carbon, through the patient teeth that ate
a thousand boards into a thousand walls.
The tools do not remember him. They don't
remember anything. That work is mine —
to hold them, to be held, and to pass on
this iron inheritance, still warm with use.

## VIII. Chains

link
&nbsp;&nbsp;by
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;link
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;we
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;are
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;made —

each one
a small decision
hardened into iron,
each one pulling
the next one tight.

Some chains we choose.
Some choose us.
Some are pretty —
gold around the throat
of a woman who paid for it herself.
Some are not —
forged onto the ankle
of a man who did not.

The same word.
The same metal.
The same physics.
Different stories.

The link does not know
what it holds together,
or apart.
It only knows
its neighbors.

That is also true
of us.

*— a brief meditation on permanence, weight, and the cold company of forged things.*
